This post highlights a concept popularized by philosopher Alan Watts and further explored by author Mark Manson known as "The Backwards Law." At its core, the law suggests that human satisfaction is often counter-intuitive: the harder we chase a positive state, the more we emphasize the void where that state should be. By desperately wanting to be happy, wealthy, or loved, you are inadvertently reinforcing a subconscious narrative that you are currently unhappy, poor, or lonely. This constant pursuit creates a psychological "negative experience" because it keeps the brain focused on what is missing, rather than what is present.
​Conversely, the second half of the law posits that accepting a negative experience is a positive experience. This is not about wallowing in misery, but rather about the relief of surrender. When you accept that you are frustrated, anxious, or flawed, the "fight" against those feelings ends. This acceptance removes the secondary layer of suffering—the "suffering about suffering"—which paradoxically leads to a state of peace.
​The post serves as a sharp critique of the modern "positivity at all costs" culture. It suggests that our fixation on self-improvement can often become a self-defeating loop; by making "enlightenment" or "perfection" a goal to be hunted, we become more self-centered and anxious. The ultimate "mind-fuck" the text refers to is the realization that true contentment doesn't come from acquiring the "positive," but from developing the emotional resilience to coexist with the "negative." It’s a call to stop running and start being, suggesting that the "night" is only scary because we refuse to let our eyes adjust to the dark.
